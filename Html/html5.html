The story of the universe begins approximately 13.8 billion years ago with the event known as the Big Bang. This colossal explosion is believed to have created all matter and energy, marking the beginning of time and space as we understand them. Before the Big Bang, the universe was in a state of infinite density and temperature, a singularity where the laws of physics as we know them break down. In the moments following the Big Bang, the universe underwent a rapid expansion, cooling as it grew. This expansion allowed for the formation of the first subatomic particles and, eventually, simple atoms. As the universe continued to expand and cool, these atoms began to coalesce under the influence of gravity, forming the first stars and galaxies. Over billions of years, these stars have forged heavier elements in their cores, which have been scattered across the universe by supernova explosions, seeding the cosmos with the building blocks of planets, life, and everything we observe today.The universe is an intricate tapestry of matter and energy, woven together in a vast cosmic web. This web is composed of galaxies, galaxy clusters, and superclusters, interconnected by filaments of dark matter and separated by enormous voids of empty space. Galaxies are the fundamental building blocks of the universe, each containing billions or even trillions of stars, along with gas, dust, and dark matter. Galaxies come in various shapes and sizes, from the majestic spirals like our own Milky Way to the elliptical galaxies, which are more spherical or oval in shape. There are also irregular galaxies, which lack a distinct form and are often shaped by gravitational interactions with other galaxies. These galaxies are not isolated but are part of larger structures such as clusters and superclusters, which are bound together by gravity on a massive scale. Beyond the galaxies, the universe is filled with dark matter and dark energy, two of the most enigmatic components of the cosmos. Dark matter, which does not emit, absorb, or reflect light, makes up about 27% of the universe's mass-energy content. Its presence is inferred from its gravitational effects on visible matter, such as the rotation curves of galaxies. Dark energy, on the other hand, is a mysterious force driving the accelerated expansion of the universe, accounting for about 68% of the universe's total energy.
